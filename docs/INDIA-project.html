Business Analysis - Indian Airlines

Python data analysis of Airline market in india

January 6, 2026 Federico Nardoni

This thesis examines the pricing mechanisms within the Indian civil aviation sector through a quantitative approach leveraging data science techniques in a Python environment. Utilizing a multi-airline dataset, the research explores the interplay between operational variables and market demand, employing linear regression models to quantify the impact of factors such as flight duration, booking lead time, cabin class, and the number of stops. The findings delineate the competitive positioning of major carriers and provide a data-driven framework for understanding price elasticity and market entry strategies within a highly competitive aviation environment.

Data Analysis of vast dataset to address pricing policy of an Indian Airlines firm

This project analyzes the major players in the Indian airline industry to identify market segmentation, structural features, and growth opportunities. MULTILINEAR REGRESSION- OLS MODEL

Predictive vs. Inferential Modeling

Our strategic objective is to develop a multiple linear regression model that utilizes inferential statistics to uncover the key relationships that drove pricing behavior in the past. In addition to this, I chose to incorporate a predictive component to the model, aiming to forecast future price movements rather than solely analyzing historical data.

Consequently, this stage of the project is divided into two distinct parts: the use of scikit-learn for predictive modeling and statsmodels for inferential analysis.

The fundamental difference between these two libraries lies in their core approach. Predictive modeling (via scikit-learn) focuses on training a model to answer the question: "How is the market likely to behave in the future, given the data provided?" In contrast, inferential analysis (via statsmodels) focuses on understanding the underlying patterns within historical data to study the specific relationships between variables.

Part I: Predictive Regression Model

Focusing on machine learning with scikit-learn to forecast future price behaviors.

The first stage of this work is articulated through the use of scikit-learn, focusing on predictive regression. The primary objective is to train a model that can answer the fundamental question: "How is the price likely to behave in a future scenario, considering the data provided?".

Unlike the inferential part, this predictive approach aims to minimize error and maximize accuracy in forecasting, rather than simply studying historical relations among variables.

Predictive Modeling

I began by plotting the relationships between key variables and fares. In this model, the dependent variable (y) is the price of fares in Rupees, while the set of explanatory variables includes all other relevant features in the dataset.

By examining these scatterplots, we can determine if the data follows a specific trend. If a non-linear relationship is observed, it may be necessary to either move away from a multiple linear regression model or perform data transformations (such as logarithmic or polynomial adjustments) to ensure the model remains valid. Evaluation of the Predictive Model and Logarithmic Transformation

Despite a strong RÂ², the initial model exhibits a high Mean Absolute Error (MAE). Specifically, on an average ticket price of 20,495 â‚¹ (~193.73 â‚¬), the model shows an average error of approximately 42-43 â‚¬. This imprecision is primarily driven by the extreme heterogeneity of the dataset, which encompasses vastly different price points across Business and Economy classes.

A key limitation of the current approach is the assumption of linearity. In the airline industry, pricing dynamics are non-linear; for instance, as the departure date approaches, prices often increase exponentially rather than linearly to capitalize on last-minute demand. Standard normalization (like Z-score) would prove counterproductive here, as it would suppress the natural and meaningful variance of the data.

Strategic Adjustment: To stabilize the variance and mitigate heteroskedasticity, I implemented a Logarithmic Transformation on the dependent variable (y).

By modeling airfares in their logarithmic form, we shift the interpretation of prediction errors into relative (percentage) terms. While this prevents a direct linear interpretation of the Beta coefficients, it significantly enhances the model's reliability.

Results after Log Transformation:

  • RÂ² Improvement: The R-squared increased to nearly 0.90, indicating a better fit due to reduced variance.
  • Error Reduction: The Mean Squared Error (MSE) dropped considerably, yielding an average prediction error of approximately 19% on the test set.

This transformation is a necessary precursor to the inferential statistics phase. Since scikit-learn does not natively provide p-values for statistical significance, this refined, stabilized dataset will now be processed via statsmodels to conduct a formal hypothesis test on each variable.

Part II: Inferential Statistical Analysis

Transitioning from scikit-learn (Prediction) to statsmodels (Inference).

While the train-test split is ideal for evaluating predictive performance, it is less suited for a rigorous, detailed statistical inference in Python. Therefore, to achieve a more comprehensive analysis of variable significance and understand the underlying relationships, I implemented a traditional Multiple Linear Regression model using the statsmodels library.

Inferential Modeling

After appropriate data preparationâ€”including the logarithmic transformation of the dependent variable to stabilize varianceâ€”I utilized the OLS (Ordinary Least Squares) method. This approach allows for the calculation of p-values, standard errors, and confidence intervals, which are essential for validating the statistical impact of each feature on airfares.

Therefore, to prove the solidity of the results obtained, I proeeded in testing the 5 hyphothesis of the Gauss-Markov OLS theorem, for an OLS model

CLASSICAL LINEAR REGRESSION MODEL ASSUMPTIONS

  1. 1. NO MULTICOLLINEARITY (VIF)

    Explanatory variables must not be highly correlated with each other. This condition was verified using the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) to ensure that each feature provides unique and independent information to the model.

  2. 2. LINEARITY

    There must be a linear relationship between the dependent variable (Price) and the regression parameters. The logarithmic transformation was specifically applied to linearize relationships that were inherently exponential.

  3. 3. NORMALITY OF RESIDUALS

    The error terms (residuals) should follow a normal distribution. This ensures that the parameter estimates are reliable and that the calculated p-values are valid for statistical inference.

  4. 4. HOMOSKEDASTICITY

    The variance of the error terms must be constant across all observations. Since variance often increases with price in flight data, the Log-Price model was adopted to stabilize this variance and mitigate heteroskedasticity.

  5. 5. NO AUTOCORRELATION

    Residuals must be independent of one another. In our dataset, each flight is treated as an independent observation to avoid biases in significance testing often found in time-series data.

Assumption #3

Normality of Residuals & Central Limit Theorem

The Jarque-Bera test from the OLS output indicates a departure from perfect normality. This is visually confirmed by the Q-Q Plot, which exhibits a heavier right tail. This pattern suggests the presence of outliers and extreme price values that influence the overall distribution of residuals.

Nonetheless, from a statistical standpoint, our analysis benefits from an exceptionally large dataset (over 80,000 observations). According to the Central Limit Theorem (CLT), as the sample size increases, the distribution of the parameter estimates tends toward normality regardless of the shape of the error distribution in the population.

This asymptotic normality allows us to rely on our p-values and t-tests for inference. In such "Big Data" scenarios, strict adherence to the normality of residuals is less critical because the model's estimators remain unbiased and consistent. Therefore, while the behavior is not perfectly Gaussian, the model is theoretically robust enough for sound strategic conclusions.

Figure: Q-Q Plot illustrating the heavy tail distribution of residuals.

Assumption #4

Homoschedasticity

Homoskedasticity implies constant variance of the error term across all levels of the independent variables. In a well-fitted model, the observable errors should remain stable without remarkable spikes or patterns. A lack of homoskedasticity (known as heteroskedasticity) may invalidate the reliability of p-values, potentially labeling variables as statistically significant when they are not.

To evaluate this, we use the Scale-Location plot. On the Y-axis, we plot the square root of standardized residuals in absolute terms. We focus on the absolute value because we are interested in the magnitude of the error rather than its direction.

The use of standardized residuals is essential here, as it allows for an easier comparison of residuals across different scales in standard measurement terms. Ideally, the red trend line in this graph should be horizontal; a steep slope or a "funnel" shape would indicate that the model's predictive precision varies significantly depending on the flight price, a common challenge in high-volatility sectors like the airline industry.

Figure: Final result of Homoschedasticity reached.

Assumption #5

Autocorrelation of Residuals

Autocorrelation occurs when the residuals (errors) of a model are correlated with each other, which is a common issue in time-series data. This phenomenon can lead to underestimated standard errors, potentially causing us to declare a variable as statistically significant when it is actually not.

To test for this, we utilize the Durbin-Watson statistic provided in the OLS output. The Durbin-Watson value ranges from 0 to 4, where a value of 2.0 indicates no autocorrelation. In our model, the test reached a value of 1.069.

While values below 2 generally suggest some degree of positive autocorrelation, in large datasets like the Air India one, this result is often considered manageable. It indicates that while there is some sequence-based dependency in the flight data, it does not critically compromise the inferential power of our regression or the validity of our Beta coefficients.

Extra Assumption

Outliers & Influence Analysis (Residuals vs Leverage)

The final stage of our diagnostic process involves the Residuals vs. Leverage plot. In this context, leverage measures how far an observation's independent variable values are from their respective means.

A high-leverage point represents an extreme or unusual observationâ€”for instance, a flight with 5 stops in a dataset where the vast majority have only 1 or 2. These points are critical because they can act as "anchor points," disproportionately pulling the regression line and potentially biasing the entire model.

Methodological Choice: I deliberately limited data transformations to the minimum necessary. While further cleaning might yield a perfectly "statistically compliant" model, over-standardizing a dataset that represents a real market segment risks creating an economically unreliable pricing strategy. Altering the data structure purely to satisfy Gauss-Markov assumptions can mask the true underlying dynamics of the airline industry.

Consequently, this is the only condition of the traditional model that is not fully satisfied. To monitor this, I employed:

  • Standardized Residuals: To express prediction errors in terms of standard deviations.
  • Cookâ€™s Distance: Represented by red dashed contours. These boundaries help identify observations exerting excessive influence. Points falling outside these contours were investigated as potential data-entry errors or extreme anomalies rather than standard market behavior.

2. Sustainable Learning & Leisure

The two Afternoon Slots provide exposure to the broader "Leisure" and mixed-demand segments. Given the non-linear price behavior observed (exponential increases near departure), afternoon slots allow the client to experiment with dynamic pricing strategies across a more diverse passenger base.

3. Hedging Against Market Saturation

With most destinations already served by 5-6 competitors, a pure "price war" would be unsustainable. This 1+2 slot configuration acts as a risk-mitigation strategy: the morning slot secures stable high-margin revenue, while the afternoon slots build market presence and volume.
